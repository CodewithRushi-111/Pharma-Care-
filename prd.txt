1. Executive Summary
The AI-Powered Smart Pharmacy and Telemedicine Platform is a unified web-based healthcare ecosystem that merges online pharmacy operations, doctor appointment management, secure video-based telemedicine, and an AI conversational assistant into a single product. The platform is designed to reduce the friction patients experience today: fragmented apps for ordering medicine, separate portals for booking doctors, and no single place to retrieve past prescriptions or consultation history.
This document defines the product scope, functional and non-functional requirements, system architecture, data model, user flows, API surface, and delivery milestones needed to design, build, and evaluate the platform as an IEEE internship deliverable. It is intended to be read by the engineering team building the product, the mentor/reviewer evaluating the internship output, and any stakeholder assessing feasibility.
1.1 Product Vision
To become a single trusted digital front door for a patient's healthcare journey — from symptom-related questions and medicine information, to booking a doctor, attending a consultation, receiving a digital prescription, and ordering the prescribed medicine — all inside one platform, guided by an AI assistant that is transparent about its limitations.
1.2 Problem Statement
Patients today face three compounding problems:
Information gap: patients lack an easy, reliable way to check medicine usage, dosage, precautions, and side effects before or after a doctor visit.
Fragmentation: pharmacy ordering, doctor booking, and consultation happen on different, disconnected platforms, causing repeated data entry and lost history.
Access friction: booking a relevant specialist, tracking an order, or retrieving an old prescription is slow, manual, and often requires a phone call or an in-person visit.
The proposed platform addresses all three by combining an AI assistant, an e-pharmacy module, and a telemedicine module under one authenticated account with a shared health record.
1.3 Target Users
Patients / End Users — individuals seeking medicine information, ordering prescriptions, or booking doctor consultations.
Doctors / Healthcare Providers — professionals managing their availability, conducting video consultations, and issuing digital prescriptions.
Pharmacy Administrators — staff managing medicine inventory, verifying prescriptions, and processing orders.
Platform Administrators — internal team managing users, doctors, catalog data, and monitoring system health.

2. Scope
2.1 In Scope (Version 1.0 / Internship Deliverable)
Patient-facing web application (responsive, desktop + mobile browser).
AI Healthcare Assistant chatbot (medicine information, symptom-to-specialization guidance, FAQ, navigation help).
Medicine catalog, search, cart, checkout, and prescription upload for regulated (Schedule H / prescription-required) medicines.
Order tracking with status lifecycle (placed → verified → packed → shipped → delivered).
Doctor directory with specialization/availability filters.
Appointment booking, rescheduling, cancellation, and automated reminders (email/SMS simulation acceptable for prototype).
WebRTC-based one-to-one video consultation room.
Digital prescription generation by doctor post-consultation, stored against the patient record.
Digital health record: consultation history, prescriptions, past orders, viewable/downloadable by the patient.
Role-based authentication for Patient, Doctor, Pharmacy Admin, and Platform Admin.
Basic admin dashboard for medicine inventory and doctor/user management.
2.2 Out of Scope (Explicitly Deferred)
Real-time payment gateway settlement with a live payment processor (a sandbox/mock payment flow is acceptable for the prototype).
Regulatory certification / legal licensing as a pharmacy (e.g., government drug-license registration) — the platform is a technology demonstrator, not a licensed pharmacy at this stage.
Native iOS/Android mobile apps (responsive web only in this phase).
Multi-language localization beyond English.
Insurance claim processing and integration.
AI-driven diagnosis or treatment prescription — the AI assistant strictly provides informational support, never a diagnosis or treatment decision (see Section 6.5 and Section 11 for safety constraints).
2.3 Assumptions
Doctors onboarded onto the platform are pre-verified by the Pharmacy/Platform Admin outside of the AI assistant's scope.
Users have a stable internet connection sufficient for WebRTC video (minimum ~1 Mbps upload/download).
The Gemini 2.5 Flash API is used strictly for informational Q&A and semantic search assistance, not as a medical decision-maker.
2.4 Constraints
Internship program timeline — the delivery plan (Section 13) assumes a constrained build window and phases features accordingly.
Team likely to be a small group of student developers — architecture favors a modular monolith over microservices for maintainability (see Section 7).
No dedicated DevOps/SRE role assumed — cloud services chosen (Firebase/AWS) favor managed infrastructure over self-hosted ops.

4. Core User Flows
4.1 Flow: AI-Assisted Medicine Discovery & Order
Patient opens AI Healthcare Assistant and asks a question (e.g., "What is this medicine used for and can I take it with paracetamol?").
Assistant returns informational guidance and a clear disclaimer to consult a pharmacist/doctor for personal medical advice, then surfaces relevant catalog items.
Patient adds medicine to cart; if the item is prescription-only, system prompts for prescription upload.
Patient uploads prescription image/PDF; order enters "Pending Verification" status.
Pharmacy Admin reviews and approves/rejects the prescription.
On approval, payment is captured (mock/sandbox) and order proceeds to packing → shipped → delivered, with status visible to the patient throughout.
4.2 Flow: Doctor Discovery, Booking & Teleconsultation
Patient searches doctors by specialization, availability, and rating.
Patient selects an open slot and confirms appointment; system sends confirmation and schedules a reminder.
At appointment time, both patient and doctor join a secure WebRTC video room from their respective dashboards.
Doctor conducts the consultation, optionally referencing the patient's digital health record (with patient consent already granted at account level).
Doctor issues a digital prescription within the platform, which is instantly saved to the patient's health record.
Patient may proceed directly from the prescription to the medicine ordering flow (Section 4.1) with the prescription pre-attached.
4.3 Flow: New User Onboarding
User signs up via email/password or federated login (Firebase Authentication).
User selects role context implicitly as "Patient" (default); Doctor/Admin roles are provisioned internally, not self-service, to prevent impersonation.
User completes a basic health profile (age, known allergies, existing conditions) — optional but recommended, used only to improve AI assistant context and clinical relevance, never shared without consent.
User lands on the dashboard showing quick actions: Ask AI Assistant, Book a Doctor, Order Medicine, View Health Records.
4.4 Flow: Prescription Verification (Pharmacy Admin)
Admin opens the verification queue, sorted by order age (oldest first).
Admin views the uploaded prescription alongside the ordered medicine list.
Admin approves, rejects with a reason, or requests re-upload.
Patient is notified of the decision in-app and via email.

6. Non-Functional Requirements
6.1 Performance
Key screens (dashboard, catalog, doctor list) shall load within 2.5 seconds on a standard broadband connection.
API response time for read endpoints (catalog search, doctor search) shall be under 500ms at p95 for the prototype's expected load (≤ 500 concurrent users).
6.2 Scalability
Backend shall be structured in modular layers (routes/controllers/services) so individual modules (pharmacy, appointments, AI, video) can later be split into separate services without a full rewrite.
Database schema shall avoid unbounded array growth on a single document (e.g., a patient's orders are separate documents referencing the patient, not embedded in the patient document) to remain performant as data grows.
6.3 Security & Privacy
All traffic shall be served over HTTPS/TLS.
Passwords shall never be stored in plaintext; Firebase Authentication or bcrypt-hashed credentials shall be used.
Health records, prescriptions, and chat history are sensitive personal data — access shall be restricted by role and ownership at the API layer (not only hidden in the UI).
Prescription files shall be stored in access-controlled cloud storage (e.g., Firebase Storage/AWS S3) with signed, time-limited URLs rather than public links.
The platform shall aim to follow the data-protection spirit of applicable regulations (e.g., India's DPDP Act, and HIPAA-equivalent handling practices) even though full regulatory certification is out of scope for the prototype (see Section 2.2).
6.4 Availability & Reliability
Target uptime for the prototype/demo period: 99% (allows for maintenance windows during an internship timeline).
Video consultation shall degrade gracefully (audio-only fallback) if bandwidth drops below the threshold needed for video.
6.5 AI Safety & Responsible-AI Constraints
The AI assistant shall never present its output as a diagnosis, a prescription, or a substitute for a licensed clinician.
Every AI response touching medicine or symptoms shall carry a visible, non-dismissible-on-first-view disclaimer.
The system shall maintain a moderation layer that intercepts queries indicating a medical emergency or self-harm risk and responds with emergency guidance rather than routing to the general-purpose model.
AI assistant prompts and responses shall be logged (with user consent captured at signup) to support quality review and safety audits.
6.6 Usability & Accessibility
UI shall meet WCAG 2.1 AA color-contrast guidelines despite the light, premium visual theme (see design.md for the specific palette and contrast-checked combinations).
Platform shall be fully responsive across desktop, tablet, and mobile browser widths.
6.7 Maintainability
Codebase shall follow a consistent module structure (feature-based folders) with documented API contracts (OpenAPI/Swagger recommended) to support handover after the internship period.

7. System Architecture
7.1 High-Level Architecture
The system follows a modular monolith pattern for the backend — a single deployable Node.js/Express service internally organized into clearly separated modules (Auth, Pharmacy, Appointments, Telemedicine, AI Assistant, Health Records, Admin) — chosen over microservices given the team size and timeline constraints described in Section 2.4. Modules communicate through internal service functions and a shared MongoDB instance, with clear boundaries that would allow extraction into independent services in a later phase.
7.2 Component Overview
Client (React.js SPA): patient, doctor, and admin experiences served from one codebase with role-based routing/layout.
API Gateway layer (Express.js): request validation, JWT verification, rate limiting, and routing to the appropriate module.
Auth Module: wraps Firebase Authentication (or a JWT-issuing custom auth service) and issues session tokens consumed by the client.
Pharmacy Module: catalog, cart, order lifecycle, prescription verification queue.
Appointments Module: doctor availability, booking, rescheduling, reminders (via a scheduled job / cron worker).
Telemedicine Module: WebRTC signaling server (Socket.io/WebSocket-based) that brokers peer connections between patient and doctor clients; media itself flows peer-to-peer (or via a TURN server when direct peer connection fails).
AI Assistant Module: server-side proxy to the Gemini 2.5 Flash API, combined with a safety/moderation pre-filter and a retrieval step against the internal medicine knowledge base (RAG-style context injection) before calling the model.
Health Records Module: read/write access to prescriptions, consultation summaries, and order history, enforcing ownership-based access control.
Admin Module: doctor onboarding, inventory management, AI-flag review dashboard.
Database (MongoDB): primary data store — see Section 8 for schema.
Cloud Storage (Firebase Storage / AWS S3): prescription images/PDFs, profile photos, generated prescription PDFs.
Notification Service: email (e.g., via SendGrid/Nodemailer) and optional SMS provider integration for order/appointment alerts.
7.3 AI Assistant Request Flow
Client sends the user's message to POST /api/ai/chat with the JWT.
Server runs a safety pre-filter (keyword + lightweight classifier) to detect emergency/self-harm signals.
If safe, server retrieves relevant context from the internal medicine knowledge base (catalog + curated medical reference data) matching the query.
Server calls the Gemini 2.5 Flash API with a system prompt enforcing the informational-only, non-diagnostic constraint plus the retrieved context.
Server appends the mandatory disclaimer, logs the exchange, and returns the response to the client.
7.4 Deployment View
Frontend: static build deployed via Firebase Hosting or an AWS S3 + CloudFront distribution.
Backend: containerized Node.js service deployed on a managed platform (e.g., AWS Elastic Beanstalk/ECS, or Render/Railway for a faster internship-friendly setup).
Database: MongoDB Atlas (managed) to avoid self-hosting operational overhead.
TURN/STUN: a managed TURN service (e.g., Twilio STUN/TURN or coturn on a small VM) to support WebRTC connections across restrictive NATs.

9. API Surface (Representative Endpoints)
The following is a representative, non-exhaustive REST contract sufficient to plan sprint work. Full OpenAPI documentation should be maintained alongside implementation.
9.1 Auth
POST /api/auth/signup — create a patient account
POST /api/auth/login — authenticate, returns JWT
POST /api/auth/refresh — refresh token
9.2 Pharmacy
GET /api/medicines?query=&category= — search catalog
GET /api/medicines/:id — medicine detail
POST /api/orders — create order (with optional prescription file reference)
GET /api/orders/:id — order status/detail
PATCH /api/orders/:id/status — admin-only status transition
9.3 Appointments
GET /api/doctors?specialization=&date= — search doctors and availability
POST /api/appointments — book a slot
PATCH /api/appointments/:id — reschedule/cancel
9.4 Telemedicine
GET /api/appointments/:id/room-token — issue a signed room-join token
WS /signaling — WebRTC offer/answer/ICE-candidate exchange channel
9.5 AI Assistant
POST /api/ai/chat — send a message, receive assistant response (safety-filtered)
GET /api/ai/conversations/:id — retrieve conversation history
9.6 Health Records
GET /api/patients/:id/prescriptions — list prescriptions (ownership-checked)
GET /api/patients/:id/history — consolidated consultation + order timeline

10. UI/UX Requirements Summary
Full visual direction, screen-by-screen layout guidance, and the exact prompt structure for generating high-fidelity mockups in Google Stitch are provided in the companion design.md file. This section captures the product-level UI requirements the design must satisfy.
Overall theme: light, clean, premium/clinical-trustworthy aesthetic — not a playful consumer app look. Ample white space, soft shadows, rounded but restrained corner radii.
Primary navigation must expose four destinations at all times for a logged-in patient: AI Assistant, Pharmacy, Doctors/Appointments, Health Records.
The AI Assistant must be reachable from a persistent floating action button on every screen, not only its own page.
Prescription-required medicines must show a clear visual badge (e.g., an "Rx" tag) distinct from over-the-counter items.
Order status and appointment status must use a consistent stepper/timeline visual pattern across the platform.
Doctor video consultation screen must be a distraction-free, full-focus layout with call controls always visible.
All disclaimers (AI assistant, prescription requirement) must be visually distinct (e.g., a tinted info banner) but not alarming red/error styling, to avoid appearing as an error state.

13. Acceptance Criteria for MVP Sign-Off
A patient can register, ask the AI assistant a medicine question, and receive a disclaimer-qualified answer.
A patient can search a medicine, add it to cart, upload a prescription, and see the order reach "Confirmed" status after admin approval.
A patient can find a doctor, book a slot, and both patient and doctor can join a working video call at the scheduled time.
A doctor can issue a digital prescription during/after a consultation, and it appears immediately in the patient's health record.
All role-restricted data (another patient's prescriptions, another doctor's schedule) is verifiably inaccessible when tested with a different account's token.
No blocking (P0) defects are open at demo time.

14. Conclusion
This PRD translates the Smart Pharmacy and Telemedicine Platform concept into a buildable specification: a modular monolith backend, a MongoDB data model that supports the pharmacy, appointment, telemedicine, and AI assistant flows, and a phased delivery plan sized for an internship timeline. The single most important constraint threaded through every section is safety: the AI assistant is explicitly scoped as an informational aid, never a diagnostic authority, with disclaimers and a moderation layer enforced at the architecture level rather than left to prompt wording alone.
The companion design.md document translates the UI requirements in Section 10 into a concrete, premium, light-themed visual system and a ready-to-use Google Stitch generation prompt, so that engineering and design work can proceed in parallel from this same specification.
Once the team's actual database schema is available, Section 8 should be reconciled against it — the structure above is intentionally written in standard MongoDB/Mongoose conventions to make that reconciliation a direct field-mapping exercise rather than a redesign.
